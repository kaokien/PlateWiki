Cutting the ring means moving strategically to reduce the space your opponent has to work with. Instead of chasing a mover around the ring in circles, you cut diagonally to intercept them. Watch Julio Cesar Chavez or Gennady Golovkin. Both could make a twenty-foot ring feel like a closet.

Think of the ring as a clock. Your opponent is at twelve. If they move to their left, don't follow the curve. Step diagonally toward where they're heading. You're cutting the straight line. This closes distance faster and puts you between the opponent and the center of the ring... which is exactly where they want to be.

The jab is what makes ring cutting work. Without it, your opponent can just pivot out of the corner and reset. A constant jab forces them to keep their hands up and move backward instead of sideways. Throw the jab as you cut the angle. It blocks their escape route.

Chasing is what happens when a pressure fighter follows the opponent in circles without cutting. You're always one step behind, never closing the distance. It's exhausting, it's ineffective, and it puts you in position to walk into counter punches. If you catch yourself running in circles... stop. Reset to the center. And cut.
