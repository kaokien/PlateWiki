Before entering competition, you should have a minimum of six to twelve months of consistent training and regular sparring experience. You should be comfortable getting hit, able to maintain composure under pressure, and have a basic game plan you can execute when stressed. One important rule... your coach should be the one who decides when you're ready. Not you. Not your ego.

Amateur boxing is governed by USA Boxing in the United States or your country's national federation. You'll need to register, complete a pre-fight medical examination, and obtain a competition passbook that tracks your fight record. Your coach must be a licensed cornerman. And you'll need competition-approved equipment... headgear, mouthguard, cup, ten-ounce competition gloves, and approved attire.

Fight preparation typically runs eight weeks. The first four to six weeks focus on increasing intensity. More rounds of sparring, fight-pace conditioning, refining your game plan. The final two weeks taper. You reduce training volume while maintaining sharpness. The goal is to peak on fight night... not burn out during camp.

On fight night, you'll arrive, weigh in, and wait. The waiting is the hardest part. Adrenaline builds with nothing to do. When your bout is called, your coach gloves you up and walks you to the ring. Amateur bouts are three rounds of three minutes each. Scoring is based on clean punches landed.

Your first fight is not about the result. It's about proving to yourself that you can step into the ring with another trained fighter and compete. You'll learn more about yourself in nine minutes of fighting than in six months of bag work. The fighters who keep competing... are the ones who treat the first fight as a lesson, not a test.
