Both slipping and rolling take your head off the center line. But they work on completely different planes.

A slip moves your head laterally, left or right, to dodge straight punches like jabs and crosses. A roll moves your head in a U-shaped arc, underneath wide punches like hooks.

Slipping is subtle. You bend slightly at the waist and shift your weight just enough for the punch to sail past your ear. Two inches is all it takes. To slip outside a jab, shift your weight to your rear foot and let the punch pass over your lead shoulder. Keep your eyes on the opponent the entire time. Your hands stay up. This is head movement... not hand movement.

Rolling is your answer to hooks. You bend at the knees, never the waist, and let the punch fly over your head. The motion traces a U shape. Down, across, and up. When you come up on the other side, your weight is loaded for a counter hook or uppercut.

The rule is simple. Slip straight punches. Roll hooks. In a real fight, the two flow together naturally. You might slip a jab and immediately roll under the follow-up hook. Train both until they become reflexive.
