Your first sparring session won't look like a fight on television. And that's by design.

Most coaches agree on a minimum before you spar. You should throw basic combinations with decent form, maintain a proper guard, and move with basic footwork. For most people, that takes about two to three months of consistent training. If you can shadow box three rounds without dropping your hands or losing your stance... you're mechanically ready.

In a well-run gym, your first spar will be controlled and light. Your partner goes at about thirty to fifty percent intensity. They're not trying to hurt you. They're creating a safe environment where you can apply your skills against a real person for the first time. You'll wear headgear, a mouthguard, sixteen-ounce gloves, and a groin protector.

Getting hit in the face for the first time is a shock. Not because it hurts... light sparring with headgear is more surprising than painful. But because it breaks the illusion of control. On the heavy bag, you dictate everything. In sparring, another human is trying to outthink you. The adrenaline dump will be intense. Elevated heart rate, tunnel vision, the urge to freeze or throw wild punches. This is completely normal.

When in doubt... jab. The jab keeps your opponent at distance, disrupts their timing, and gives you something to do other than stand there. Keep your hands up. Keep moving. And breathe. Most beginners hold their breath during sparring, which accelerates fatigue. Don't try to win your first spar. Try to survive and learn.
