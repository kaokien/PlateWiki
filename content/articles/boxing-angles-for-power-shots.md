# Creating Angles: How to Position Your Feet for Maximum Power

In boxing, standing directly in front of your opponent is a recipe for mutual destruction. If you trade punches on the same centerline, the fight becomes a test of chin resilience and raw power. 

To become an elite boxer, you must fight in three dimensions. You must create angles. 

An angle is a position where you can land your punches while your opponent's weapons are pointed away from you. Creating an angle requires precise footwork. You must shift your body off the centerline, realign your hips, and drive your punches from a new vector. 

Here is the technical blueprint for positioning your feet to create dominant angles.

---

## The Geometry of the Angle

To understand angles, you must visualize the centerline. This is the straight line connecting your nose to your opponent's nose. All straight punches travel along this line.

```
       [ Opponent ]
            |
            |  <-- Centerline (Line of Fire)
            |
         [ You ]
```

When you shift off this line, you create a new relationship between your hips and their guard. 

If you step to the outside of their lead shoulder, their lead hand can no longer hit you effectively, and their rear hand is blocked by their own chest. Meanwhile, your hips are aligned with their exposed side, opening a clean path for your power shots.

```
       [ Opponent ]
           /
          /   <-- New Vector of Attack
         /
     [ You ]  (Positioned at an Angle)
```

Power in boxing comes from rotation. If you step to an angle but do not realign your feet, you cannot rotate your hips. Your punches will be arm-punches, lacking force. To hit with power, your feet must plant and face the target at the end of your movement.

---

## Technique 1: The Outside Step and Pivot (The T-Angle)

This is the most common angle-creation method. It is used to slip inside or outside the opponent's jab and secure a position on their flank.

### The Footwork
1.  As the opponent throws a jab, slip your head to the outside (your right, assuming orthodox vs orthodox).
2.  Simultaneously, step your lead (left) foot forward and to the left, placing it outside the opponent's lead foot.
3.  Transfer your weight to the ball of your lead foot.
4.  Pivot your rear (right) foot in a 45-degree arc to the left.
5.  Your chest and hips should now point directly at the opponent's right ear. You are standing perpendicular to their stance.

### The Punch Selection
This angle opens a clear lane for the straight cross or the rear uppercut. Because you are standing on their side, their guard is facing forward, leaving their temple and ribs completely exposed. Drive your right hand straight through their ear or ribcage.

---

## Technique 2: The Inside Pivot (The Slip-Step)

This technique is highly effective when facing aggressive fighters who march straight forward. You let their momentum carry them past you, then hit them from behind.

### The Footwork
1.  Slip to the inside of the incoming punch (your left).
2.  Step your rear (right) foot out to the right side, widening your stance.
3.  Pivot on the ball of your lead foot, swinging your rear heel around so your body turns 90 degrees to face the opponent's flank.
4.  Keep your weight centered. Do not lean over your knees.

### The Punch Selection
This inside pivot loads your lead hip. The moment your rear foot plants, explode with a lead hook to the body (targeting their spleen or liver) or a lead hook to the chin. The opponent is still moving forward, so they will run directly into the path of your hook.

---

## Technique 3: The Cutback (The L-Step Shift)

The cutback is a deceptive movement that changes the rhythm of the fight. It is used to escape pressure and immediately transition into an attack.

### The Footwork
1.  Step your rear foot back to escape a forward charge.
2.  Instead of sliding your lead foot straight back, step it laterally to your left.
3.  Push off your rear foot to shift your body weight onto your lead foot.
4.  Your feet are now aligned at an angle to the opponent's line of retreat.
5.  Plant your feet and rotate your hips.

### The Punch Selection
The lateral shift of the L-step creates a path for the overhand right or the straight cross. The opponent expects you to continue moving backward. When you suddenly shift sideways and plant your feet, you catch them off-balance as they try to reset their stance.

---

## The Importance of Hip Alignment

Many fighters execute the footwork for an angle but fail to land with power because they throw their punches while their hips are still turning. 

You must separate the movement phase from the punch phase:
1.  **Move:** Step and pivot to the new angle.
2.  **Plant:** Anchor the balls of both feet to the canvas.
3.  **Rotate:** Pivot on your feet to turn your hips into the punch.

If you punch during the move phase, you will slide off-balance. The canvas is your source of power. You must connect with the ground to transfer force through your legs, hips, or shoulders.

## Gym Drills for Angle Creation

To build these footwork patterns into your combat system, practice these structured drills:

### The Ring Line Drill
Find a straight line on the gym floor (such as the edge of the ring canvas). 
*   Stand facing the line in your boxing stance.
*   Practice stepping across the line with your lead foot, pivoting 90 degrees, and throwing a 1-2 combination.
*   Reset and repeat. The line acts as a visual centerline, ensuring your footwork takes you completely off the path of an imaginary opponent's punch.

### The Partner Blind-Spot Drill
Have a partner stand in front of you holding target pads. 
*   Your partner will throw a slow, straight punch.
*   You must slip, execute one of the three angle-creation footwork patterns, and land a two-punch combination on the pads.
*   Your partner must remain stationary until you punch. This helps you verify that you are standing in their blind spot (perpendicular to their chest) before you hit.
*   Gradually increase the speed of the partner's punch as your timing improves.
