Weight cutting is part of competitive boxing at every level. Fighters typically compete at a weight lower than their natural walking-around weight. Understanding how it works, and how to do it safely, is essential for anyone planning to compete.

Weight management has two distinct phases. The long-term phase, eight to twelve weeks out, gradually reduces body fat through a small caloric deficit. One to two pounds per week. The goal is to arrive at fight week within five to eight pounds of your target while keeping your strength and energy.

The fight-week phase manipulates water and sodium intake. A common approach is water loading. You drink large amounts of water early in the week, over two gallons per day, to signal your body to increase urine output. Then you gradually reduce water intake over the final twenty-four to thirty-six hours. Your body keeps flushing at the elevated rate even as intake drops... resulting in temporary water weight loss. This must be done carefully with someone experienced.

Here's the important part. Extreme weight cutting is dangerous. Sauna suits, excessive saunas, laxatives, and complete water restriction can lead to kidney damage, heat stroke, and in extreme cases, death. Several fighters have died from weight-cutting complications. If you need to cut more than eight percent of your body weight in the final week... you're fighting at the wrong weight class. Move up. Your health is worth more than a size advantage.

After weigh-in, the priority is replenishing fluids and energy. Sip electrolyte-rich fluids rather than chugging water. Eat small, easily digestible meals. By fight night, you should feel fully recovered... not depleted.
