The one-two combination has ended more fights than any other sequence in the sport. It works because the first punch sets up the second. Your jab finds the range and blinds your opponent. Then the cross arrives through the opening... before they can even react. 

It's not two separate punches. It's one fluid sequence. As your jab returns, you pivot your rear foot and rotate your hips. The cross drives straight down the center line. 

Here's a pro tip. Change the rhythm. Throw the jab at a measured speed, then accelerate the cross. Think of it as a tap... then a bang. The change in speed makes it incredibly hard to defend. 

Don't just throw it in the open air, set it up. Throw a few single jabs first, then let the cross follow. And remember, the one-two is just a launching pad. Follow it with a lead hook, or step off the angle. But never stand in the pocket after throwing.
